# Writing Craft

> [!info] Purpose
> Principles and frameworks for clear, persuasive prose. Used for: [[Projects/Q3-Product-Launch]] onboarding copy, [[Projects/Podcast-Series]] scripts, [[Areas/Career-Development]] performance reviews and executive memos.

## Core Principles

1. **Lead with the point** (inverted pyramid). The reader's most important question is "why does this matter to me?" — answer it in sentence 1.
2. **One idea per paragraph.** If you need to split a paragraph, you probably have two ideas.
3. **Sentences under 25 words.** Long sentences hide weak thinking.
4. **Use verbs, not nouns.** "We decided" > "a decision was made." "It reduces cost" > "it leads to cost reduction."
5. **Delete adverbs.** "Very", "really", "quite" — nearly always cuttable.
6. **Show, don't tell** — in narratives. For business writing: tell, then show (data / example).

## Frameworks

### The Minto Pyramid (exec memos)
1. Recommendation / conclusion first
2. 3 supporting arguments
3. Evidence under each argument
4. Appendix for detail

### The Narrative Arc (onboarding / blog)
1. **Before** — the world with the problem
2. **Conflict** — what makes solving it hard
3. **After** — the world once solved
4. **Bridge** — how you get from before to after

Applied to [[Projects/Q3-Product-Launch]] onboarding:
- Before: async teams lose context in scattered tools
- Conflict: switching between apps breaks flow and creates version chaos
- After: Meridian's new async layer keeps everyone aligned in one place
- Bridge: 5-minute setup, migrates your existing workflow

### The Rule of Three
Group into threes for memorability. "Fast, flexible, and affordable" > "Fast, flexible, affordable, and reliable."

## Style Guide (personal)

- Oxford comma: always
- Numbers: spell out one–nine, use numerals for 10+
- Em dashes: use for sharp pivots, not as parentheses substitute
- Passive voice: maximum 10% of sentences
- Reading level target: Flesch–Kincaid Grade 9
