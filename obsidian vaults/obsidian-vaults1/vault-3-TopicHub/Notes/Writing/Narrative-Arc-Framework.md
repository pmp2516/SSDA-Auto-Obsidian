# Narrative Arc Framework

> The before/conflict/after/bridge structure. Universally applicable to product writing, presentations, essays, and podcast scripts.

## The Four Parts

### 1. Before
The world as it exists with the problem present. Concrete and specific — not "people waste time" but "the average knowledge worker spends 2.6 hours/day searching for information they've already found."

The goal: make the reader *feel* the problem before you name it.

### 2. Conflict
What makes solving the problem hard. This is where most writers shortchange — they jump from problem to solution without honouring the difficulty.

Conflict creates investment. If the problem were easy, you wouldn't need this product / idea / approach. The conflict validates the reader's frustration and sets up your solution as genuinely necessary.

### 3. After
The world once the problem is solved. Be specific and vivid. Not "teams are more productive" but "engineers find the context they need in 30 seconds, stay in flow, and leave work on time."

After ≠ features. After = how the reader's life is different.

### 4. Bridge
How you get from Before to After. This is where you introduce the mechanism — the product, the idea, the framework.

Bridge comes last because the reader is now primed. They've felt the problem, respected the difficulty, imagined the outcome. Now they're ready to hear the "how."

## Applied to Meridian v2.0 Onboarding

- **Before**: "When your team spans 3 time zones, async discussions get buried in email threads and Slack channels nobody searches."
- **Conflict**: "Collaboration tools help, but they each have their own inbox. The context is split."
- **After**: "With Meridian's async layer, decisions stay attached to the work they're about. New team members can read the full history in minutes, not days."
- **Bridge**: "Set up takes 5 minutes. Here's how."

## Applied to Podcast Episode (Kahneman)

- **Before**: "You think you're making rational risk assessments. You're not."
- **Conflict**: "The problem isn't information — you can Google the statistics. But knowing the statistics and *feeling* safe on a plane are entirely different experiences."
- **After**: "Once you see the mechanism, you can't unsee it. Your decisions won't be perfect, but they'll be more deliberate."
- **Bridge**: "The mechanism is called the availability heuristic. Here's the original experiment."
