# perm-0045 — The availability heuristic: definition and mechanism

**Core idea**: People judge the frequency or probability of an event by how easily examples come to mind. The ease of retrieval is mistaken for evidence of frequency.

This is a *substitution* heuristic: a hard question ("what is the base rate of X?") is unconsciously replaced by an easy one ("how quickly can I think of an example of X?").

**Why it persists**: Because availability is correlated with frequency under normal conditions — common things do leave more memory traces. The heuristic is therefore adaptive in typical environments; it fails badly when availability is determined by salience, recency, emotional vividness, or media exposure rather than actual frequency.

**Structural cause**: System 1 processing (fast, automatic) generates availability estimates without deliberate calculation. System 2 (slow, deliberate) would correct the error — but it is often not engaged.

## Implications

1. Judgments made under time pressure or cognitive load are more availability-biased (System 2 unavailable).
2. Providing statistics rarely corrects availability bias because the *feeling* of ease overrides the numbers.
3. Pre-mortems and checklists are debiasing tools precisely because they force System 2 engagement before a decision.
