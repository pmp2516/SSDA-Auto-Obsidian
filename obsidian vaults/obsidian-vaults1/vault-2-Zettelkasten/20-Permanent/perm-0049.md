# perm-0049 — Base-rate neglect: vivid scenarios suppress statistical priors

**Core idea**: When a vivid, specific scenario is available, people discount or ignore the statistical base rate for that class of events.

**Classic demonstration (Kahneman)**: Given a description of a woman named Linda — "bright, politically active, concerned about social justice" — and asked whether Linda is more likely to be (a) a bank teller or (b) a bank teller who is active in the feminist movement, most people choose (b). But (b) is a subset of (a); it must be less probable.

The specific description makes (b) feel more "representative," and this representativeness overrides the statistical logic. This is related to — but distinct from — availability: it's about the match between description and prototype, not ease of recall.

**Relationship to availability**: Both involve substitution of an intuitive judgment for a statistical one. Availability substitutes "ease of recall" for frequency; base-rate neglect substitutes "representativeness" for prior probability. They often co-occur and reinforce each other.

**Debiasing**: Consider the base rate explicitly before encountering the specific case. "Before I look at this candidate's CV, what fraction of candidates in this pipeline are typically hireable?" Anchoring on the prior reduces but does not eliminate the effect.
