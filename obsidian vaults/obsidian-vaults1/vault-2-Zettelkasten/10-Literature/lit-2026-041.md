# lit-2026-041 — Availability Heuristic (Kahneman, Chapter 12)

## Source

Kahneman, "Thinking, Fast and Slow," Chapter 12. See also parent paper: [[lit-2026-038]] (Tversky & Kahneman, 1973).

## Summary

Kahneman explains that availability is a heuristic by which we substitute a hard question ("how frequent is X?") with an easier one ("how easily can I recall an example of X?"). The ease of retrieval — not the actual evidence — drives the judgment.

**Central claim**: Availability is experienced as an emotion, not a calculation. When examples come to mind easily, they *feel* more probable. This is a feature of System 1 processing.

**Risk perception example**: After a plane crash is widely reported, people overestimate the probability of dying in a plane crash. The images are vivid and available; car crashes, which kill vastly more people, generate less media coverage and therefore feel safer.

**Cascade effects**: Public availability of risk judgments can cascade — media reports increase fear → people demand regulation → politicians respond to perceived risk, not actuarial risk.

## My Reading

The chapter extends the 1973 paper in an important direction: it shows that availability bias is not just about memory retrieval but about the emotional experience of retrieval. This is why it's so hard to debias simply by providing statistics — the feeling of ease overrides the numbers.

For the podcast, this gives me a strong emotional hook: "You think you're making a rational judgment about risk. You're actually making a judgment about how easy it was to imagine something bad happening."
