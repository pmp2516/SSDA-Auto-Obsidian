# lit-2026-038 — Availability: A Heuristic for Judging Frequency and Probability

## Source

Tversky & Kahneman, 1973 — the original paper establishing the availability heuristic. Parent of [[lit-2026-041]].

## Summary

The paper demonstrates that people assess the frequency or probability of an event by the ease with which instances come to mind. This is the "availability heuristic." The authors ran multiple experiments showing that this heuristic produces systematic and predictable biases.

**Key experiment**: Participants estimated whether more words in English begin with the letter K or have K as the third letter. Most said "begins with K," but in reality far more words have K as the third letter. Words starting with K are easier to recall, so availability inflates the estimate.

**Implication**: Availability is a cognitive shortcut, not a statistical tool. It works reasonably well in typical conditions (common events leave more traces in memory) but fails badly when availability is determined by salience, recency, or emotional vividness rather than actual frequency.

## My Reading

This is the experimental foundation for a large swath of behavioural economics. Kahneman later synthesised this in "Thinking, Fast and Slow" as the System 1 / cognitive ease framework.

The K-word experiment is beautifully simple. I want to use it in the Podcast Ep 1 as an accessible demonstration of the principle before moving to higher-stakes examples (risk perception, news media).
