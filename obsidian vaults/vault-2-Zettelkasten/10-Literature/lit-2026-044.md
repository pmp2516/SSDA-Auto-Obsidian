# lit-2026-044 — Deep Work (Newport)

## Source

Cal Newport, "Deep Work" (2016).

## Summary

Newport argues that the ability to perform "deep work" — cognitively demanding tasks in a state of distraction-free concentration — is becoming both increasingly rare and increasingly valuable. Two rules:

1. Deep work is valuable (produces cognitive artefacts that shallow work cannot)
2. Deep work is rare (the connected workplace systematically erodes it)

**The depth hypothesis**: In the new economy, the ability to quickly master hard things and produce at an elite level are the two core competencies. Both require deep work.

**Scheduling philosophy**: Don't hope for focus; schedule it. Newport advocates four scheduling philosophies: Monastic, Bimodal, Rhythmic, Journalistic. Rhythmic is most practical for employed knowledge workers — same time each day, non-negotiable.

## My Reading

This book changed my morning routine more concretely than any other. The "Any benefit" fallacy about social media was eye-opening — I had been justifying Twitter as a "networking tool" while it was clearly fragmenting my attention.

Practical implementation I use (from Appendix B-style notes):
- 08:00–12:00 protected deep work daily
- Phone in another room until noon
- Shutdown ritual: review tomorrow, clear inbox, say "shutdown complete" aloud
