# lit-2026-050 — Noise: A Flaw in Human Judgment (Kahneman, Sibony, Sunstein)

## Source

Kahneman, Sibony & Sunstein (2021). Companion volume to Kahneman's earlier work. Sibling to [[lit-2026-041]].

## Summary

The book distinguishes **bias** (systematic error in one direction) from **noise** (random scatter in judgments). Both degrade decision quality, but organisations focus almost entirely on bias while ignoring noise.

**Key distinction**:
- *Bias*: judges who consistently sentence minorities more harshly (systematic, directional)
- *Noise*: two equally experienced judges give radically different sentences to identical defendants, depending on whether their local sports team won the previous day (random, unpredictable)

**Occasion noise vs. level noise**: Occasion noise is random variation in a single judge's decisions across time (mood, fatigue, what they ate). Level noise is consistent differences between judges (one judge is simply harsher than another).

**The noise audit**: The book proposes a diagnostic tool — show the same case independently to multiple judges and measure the variance. The variance itself (before asking who is right) is the noise measure.

**Decision hygiene**: Practices to reduce noise: structured assessment, delay intuition until all evidence is in, use reference classes, use scales calibrated with examples.

## My Reading

This reframes a lot of my thinking about product decision-making. The PRD review process at Apex is a bias audit — we check for motivated reasoning, incomplete data, HIPPO influence. But we never measure *variability* across reviewers, which is exactly what noise auditing would catch.
