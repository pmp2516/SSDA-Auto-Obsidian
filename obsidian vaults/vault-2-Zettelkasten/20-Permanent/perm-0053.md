# perm-0053 — Intermittent reinforcement as the mechanism of attention capture

**Core idea**: Social media and messaging apps are not accidentally addictive — they are engineered using intermittent reinforcement schedules (the same psychological mechanism as slot machines) to maximise engagement by making reward unpredictable.

**The mechanism**: A fixed-ratio reinforcement schedule (every pull = reward) is easily habituated and abandoned. A *variable-ratio* schedule (reward comes unpredictably) produces the highest and most persistent response rates in both animals and humans. "Pulling to refresh" is structurally identical to pulling a slot machine lever.

**The design intent**: Tristan Harris and other former tech insiders have documented that variable-ratio reinforcement is explicitly designed into notification timing, feed ordering, and reaction features. The goal is maximising daily active use (a business metric), not user wellbeing.

**Implications for attention**:
1. Once an intermittent-reinforcement loop is established, the *checking behaviour* becomes compulsive independent of whether the user finds the content valuable.
2. The behaviour is difficult to moderate because moderation (checking less often) is also a variable schedule — you just shifted the ratio.
3. The only reliable interruption is removal, not reduction (Newport's 30-day declutter).

**Important nuance**: The problem is not the platforms per se but the *checking habit*. RSS, email, and even social media can be used without intermittent reinforcement loops if consumed on a schedule (pull at 08:00, not push throughout the day).
