# perm-0055 — Decision hygiene: structured practices to reduce noise in judgment

**Core idea**: Just as physical hygiene prevents disease by interrupting contamination pathways, "decision hygiene" is a set of structured practices that interrupt the pathways through which noise enters judgment. It does not eliminate judgment — it ensures that judgment is applied to signal, not to noise.

**Key practices** (from Kahneman, Sibony & Sunstein):

1. **Delay intuition**: Gather and structure all relevant evidence *before* forming an overall impression. Premature closure anchors on the first salient feature.

2. **Use structured assessment**: Evaluate each dimension of a decision independently (rather than a holistic impression) before combining into a conclusion. Avoids the halo effect where a strong first impression bleeds into all sub-assessments.

3. **Independent aggregation**: When multiple judges are involved, collect their individual assessments *before* discussion. Discussion converts independent evidence into correlated evidence — the dominant voice anchors everyone else.

4. **Reference class forecasting**: Before estimating any quantity (time, cost, probability), identify the reference class and its base rate. Apply the base rate as a prior, then adjust.

5. **Calibration sessions**: Before high-stakes decisions, run a "noise audit" — all reviewers independently assess the same test case, then compare. The variance is the noise level; the discussion reveals implicit criteria differences that can then be made explicit.

**Connection to availability heuristic**: Delaying intuition specifically interrupts availability bias — the most *memorable* feature is usually the first to come to mind and the hardest to discount once formed (see [[perm-0047]]).
