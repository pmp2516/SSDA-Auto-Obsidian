# perm-0047 — Availability ≠ probability: frequency judgment as a retrieval artifact

**Core idea**: When we judge how probable or frequent something is, we are often reporting the ease with which examples came to mind — a retrieval property — rather than making a statistical inference.

This is a stronger claim than just "availability heuristic." It says that frequency judgments are not fundamentally probabilistic reasoning that has gone wrong; they are a *different process altogether* — one that reports retrieval fluency while presenting itself as probability estimation.

**Consequence**: Correcting availability-based judgments with statistics is ineffective not because people are stubborn, but because the judgment was never generated by the statistical faculty. You are correcting the output of Process A by appealing to Process B — they are not in conversation.

**What does work**:
- Generating counter-examples (forces retrieval of underrepresented instances)
- Pre-mortems (structured generation of failure scenarios)
- Reference class forecasting (override availability with base rates before making the judgment)
