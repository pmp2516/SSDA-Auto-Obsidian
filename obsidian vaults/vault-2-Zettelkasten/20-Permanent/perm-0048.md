# perm-0048 — Media amplification creates asymmetric risk perception via availability cascade

**Core idea**: Mass media systematically distorts risk perception by making rare, dramatic events highly available in memory while common, mundane events receive little coverage.

**Mechanism**: Coverage volume and emotional salience increase memory availability → increased availability inflates probability estimates → inflated estimates produce fear and demand for policy response → media, politicians, and public amplify each other in a feedback loop. Kahneman calls this an "availability cascade."

**Asymmetry examples**:

| Event | Actual annual deaths (US) | Media coverage index | Perceived relative risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Terrorism | ~50 | Very high | Very high |
| Car accidents | ~42,000 | Low | Moderate |
| Medical errors | ~250,000 | Very low | Low |
| Heart disease | ~700,000 | Moderate | Moderate |

The table shows a near-inversion: media coverage and perceived risk are inversely correlated with actual mortality for several categories.

**Design implication**: When communicating product risk (bugs, security) or organisational risk (launch slippage), avoid triggering availability bias by controlling the salience of your examples. Use reference classes and base rates explicitly.
