# perm-0054 — Noise vs bias: why organisations systematically ignore the harder problem

**Core idea**: Bias (systematic directional error) and noise (random variability in judgment) both degrade decision quality, but organisations almost exclusively address bias while ignoring noise — because noise is harder to detect, politically neutral, and implies that *everyone's* judgment is unreliable, not just the villain's.

**The key distinction**:
- **Bias** has a direction and an identifiable cause. It implies someone is wrong in a consistent, blameworthy way. It creates a story.
- **Noise** has no direction. It means that equally qualified, well-intentioned professionals reach wildly different conclusions from the same evidence. It implies the *system* is unreliable, not any person.

**Why organisations avoid noise**:
1. Measuring noise requires a noise audit — showing the same case to multiple judges and measuring variance. This is politically threatening: it reveals that the organisation's decisions are less reliable than assumed.
2. Reducing noise requires *standardisation*, which judges experience as a loss of autonomy ("my professional judgment is being replaced by a checklist").
3. Bias reduction is narratively satisfying (identify the biased person, fix the bias). Noise reduction is unglamorous (calibrate everyone to a shared standard).

**The implication for product decisions**: Performance reviews, launch go/no-go decisions, and hiring panels all generate noise. A team of four reviewers scoring the same PRD will produce a variance that has nothing to do with the PRD's quality — it reflects the reviewers' different implicit reference frames. Calibration sessions before high-stakes reviews directly reduce noise.
