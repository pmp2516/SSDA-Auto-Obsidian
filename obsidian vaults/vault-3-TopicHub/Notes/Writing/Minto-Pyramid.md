# The Minto Pyramid Principle

> Conclusion-first communication framework for executive audiences. Used in every memo, Slack message, and presentation for [[../../People/Denise-Park]].

## The Core Structure

```
RECOMMENDATION / CONCLUSION (top)
    ├── Supporting argument 1
    │       └── Evidence / data
    ├── Supporting argument 2
    │       └── Evidence / data
    └── Supporting argument 3
            └── Evidence / data
```

**The rule**: State your recommendation before your reasoning. The audience can stop reading at any time and still have the most important information.

This is the opposite of how most people write (build up to the conclusion). The bottom-up structure buries the point and forces the reader to hold uncertainty while processing evidence — frustrating for busy executives.

## Why It Works for Executives

Executives are making dozens of decisions per day. They do not read to understand your reasoning process — they read to extract your recommendation and the top 2–3 reasons to believe it. If they agree, they stop reading. If they have questions, they drill down.

The pyramid gives them this choice. The narrative structure (reasoning → conclusion) denies it.

## Applied Examples

### Slack message to [[../../People/Denise-Park]]

❌ Bottom-up (avoid):
> "We reviewed the user research, spoke to Tom about technical feasibility, checked the competitive landscape, and based on all of this we think we should add workspace-level permission controls in v2.1."

✅ Pyramid (use):
> "Recommendation: add workspace-level permission controls in v2.1. Three reasons: (1) blocks 3 enterprise deals currently in pipeline, (2) technically feasible in one sprint (Tom confirmed), (3) no competitive product offers granular controls at this level."

### PRD executive summary

Lead with: what you're building, why it matters in one sentence, and the key decision you need.
